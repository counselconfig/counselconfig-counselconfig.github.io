Security Policy - Ethics, Dilemmas, and Productivity

 

 

1.   Introduction

 

1.1 Background

 

Once only inhabiting Neuromancer, cyberthreat is no longer science-fiction as we desire secure networks more than ever (Dederer & Singer, 2019). Specifically, information is driving acute defensive control, with arguably security policy (SP) the most imperative (Masrek, et al., 2020). Yet, despite its good deeds, SP comes with its own exterior problems. 

 

 

1.2 Context

 

Security threats are an information technology (IT) vice, taking less to exploit vulnerabilities than to defend (Devereux, 2019). As countermeasures are insufficient alone, SP entrusts people as weakest links to fill the protection void but with latent costs. (Rostami, et al., 2020). Contextually, we study SP risks from ethical, legal, and social literature.

 

 

1.3 Motivation

 

In two years, online devices expect threefold global population outgrowth (Hill, 2020), but not all users will be benign. The extent to which SP will tackle threats before it becomes one itself is a discussion to be had. In that regard, this paper conducts a literature review about SP questioning:

 

a)       Can SP be defined?

b)      What SP enforcement challenges are there?

c)       Why might SP be a workplace problem?

 

Thereafter, a conclusion will summarise review findings before ending with recommendations.

 

2.   Literature Review

 

2.1 Can SP be defined?

 

Scope

 

Policy is a variable term, but as a technological security, its scope interchanges with information and cyber spaces (Li & Liu, 2021; Corradini, 2020) - Figure 1: 

 

 

Figure 1 SP scope adapted (Tagarev & Polimirova, 2019)

 

Observably in Figure 1, cyber SP can be a company and state instrument (Management Association, 2021), whereas information SP is an organisational product (Sharif & Ameen, 2020). Moreover, information SP is a ‘personal data’ proprietor whereas cyber SP owns ‘digital information’. SP is not just about rules, it differs at the highest abstraction level comprising: goals, objectives, and strategy. Achieving these are undefined for enterprises to steer with documents (Maleh & Belaissaoui, 2021) – Figure 2.

 

 

 

Figure 2 SP documents for infrastructure domains– adapted (Johnson & Easttom, 2020)

 

Framework

 

If there ever was a prescribed SP definition, it would be in RFC2196: ‘a formal statement of the rules by which people who are given access to an organization’s technology and information assets must abide’ (Network Working Group, 1997). Considered a network domain in ISO/IEC 27002 (2013), SP frameworks (SPF) improve organisational cybersecurity each having pros and cons (Garcia, 2019). Notably, protecting confidentiality, integrity, and availability is a default function of SPF (Rakshitha, 2020), which may incorporate ‘authenticity, accountability, non-repudiation, and reliability’ in ISO/IEC 17799 (2005),  plus ‘assurance’ (NIST SP 800-30, 2012). Building an SP from the grounds is no straightforward investment (IT Procedure Template, 2020). SP is normally an artefact of holistic risk assessments guided by SPF (Pecheniuk, 2021) – Figure 3. 

 

 

Figure 3 SPF and development – adapted Tuyikeze & Flowerday (2014) and Peltier (2016)

 

SFP patterns backed by legislation, e.g., EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) may help sponsor enterprise strategic objectives with policies, standards, guidelines, procedures, and best practice (Quinn, 2021). Though benefits conferred, uniformity of documents is argued an infrastructural threat for reusable precedents to crack datacentre baselines exploited in the wild (LDP Associates, Inc., 2020).

 

 

2.2 What SP enforcement challenges are there?

 

Methods

 

One challenge enforcing SP beyond endpoints and servers is synchronising control of a security programme (SPGM) for staff (Cassetto, 2019). Across enterprises, SPGM differs according to network architectures, making it a managerial job matching vulnerabilities to SPF - Figure 4.

With GPDR €20 million fines imposable (Proton Technologies AG, 2021), training staff under SPGM is indispensable. Albeit, behind this educational motive are findings employees only apprehend significance in SP duties after tragic events ensue from their noncompliance   (Angraini, et al., 2019). Ethics

 

Counterintuitive it may seem to say, SP has its own threats. More precisely, it poses dangers to ethical values to which people hold a ‘positive response’ (van de Poel, 2020). It is a claim which at its cornerstone revisits a classic trade-off between conflicting values which, in SP terms, means not all values may be honoured concurrently in cybersecurity. Research in Ali, et al. (2021) suggests employees normally develop value conflicts towards SPs. This is because people are more likely to violate policies compromising their own values beliefs (Ertan, et al., 2018). In a similar vein, van de Poel (2020) points out that SP measures contesting held values are in a ‘moral dilemma’ causing second-order policy choices to be made, which placate value oppositions. The literature alludes to values with plurality manifesting in clusters: ‘accountability’, ‘fairness’, ‘privacy’, and ‘security’, the last of which protects against information and human harms resulting inter alia from crimes and cyberwarfare. However, these clusters are non-exhaustive and numerous when considering what conflicts may result from SP - Figures 9 and 10.

Dilemmas

 

Affordability, ease, and covertness are key attributes evolving cybercrime (Kassab, 2019), yet there is a chance fortifying security measures cause them to rise in the first place. It is circular progression akin to the Data Encryption Standard ‘Deep Crack’, which spurred needs for an Advanced Encryption Standard for secure network transfer protocols (Reji, et al., 2020). However, cryptography represents only a facet of the SP stack, where holistically network defences are progressing at rates that cannot keep up with sophisticated intrusion tools (Meckl, et al., 2021; Gompert & Libicki, 2021).  What this describes is a pacing problem– a broadening differential between emergent cyberattack dissemination and time for regulatory oversights to establish devolved SPs. These are words made in similar vein to Brehm’s (2019) literature on innovative weaponry against regulation as a ‘race metaphor’. Except, in the network security domain, emergent attack vectors from cyberspace feed on the back of novelty tech. It means SP only has transitory effect before its defence strategy expires to modernisation. Like new munitions, incoming technology surfacing the web offers criminals opportunities to commit progressive offences behind policy (Mahanti, 2021). Still germane, one in their seminal take on the quandary, Downes (2009) provided an anecdotal calculus declaring ‘technology changes exponentially, but social, economic, and legal systems change incrementally’, mapped in Figure 11:

 

 

Figure 11 SP pacing problem adapted (Friedman, 2017; Donaldson, et al., 2018)

 

Where pacing lacunas arise from divergences in scientific development and measures, the inaugurated technology enters the next generation mostly undetected from regulatory radars. Scholars have before espoused this point in time a ‘social experiment’ (Pieters, et al., 2014) – a phase which has hitherto been linked with Collingridge dilemma and Moore’s Law (Williams, 2021). It is because when rolled out to a populace, disruptive IT likens as social ‘betaware’ with undiscernible side-effects. But as the new technology falls to societal entrenchment, restrictions on its movement start to collapse irreversibly. Circumstantially, the problem is a ‘double-bind’, which Macnish (2022) suggests that even when new untested technologies are available, the extent to which they may be used maliciously is uncertain. A predicament which may depreciate a company’s security posture.

 

Productivity

 

A socioeconomic tug of war is at stake in the life of an employee. As one exercises labour to attain contractual performance, one must simultaneously honour rubrics governed by SP. Employees are voicing their concerns about it too with around 90% respondents recently claiming policy hinders their trade (Bongiovanni, 2020). The trouble is not necessarily subsisting bifold responsibilities but a case of compliance obligations cancelling agile executive functions (Posey & Canham, 2018). In a behavioural prioritisation study of healthcare staff, cybersecurity measures were deemed “counterproductive and time consuming” to meet critical patient demands  (Coventry, et al., 2020). Results in their research revealed circumstances where staff rejected SPs because of dogmas they held about measures burdening their productivity e.g., needing admin privilege for installations.  In Posey & Canham (2018), such convictions affect employees driven to ‘dual-process’ judgments between doing their jobs completely with some security or doing some of their job with complete security. Ironically, as the literature reveals, headlining malicious breaches from insiders only fraction overall threats. In majority cases, it says, “non-malicious noncompliance” falls afoul to SP due to productivity succumbences. These findings evidence a diametrical relationship between SP and diminishing productivity – Figure 12.

 

 

 

 

Figure 12 SP/Productivity (P) graph based on ‘diminishing returns’ (Aiken, 2019)

 

Following on, Coventry, et al. (2020) find different thoughts occupying workers may also impact their productivity, one being a lack of confidence in measures improving security. For reasons to do with negativities like these, senior management even IT personnel are known to opine the cause a ‘cultural issue’. So, productivity curbs may not be ‘technically’ linked with SP, but rather ‘socially’ inclined. Another argument appeals to accounts of ‘technostress’ diagnoses reducing productivity (Reeves, et al., 2021).  Reasons like these are attributable to rising cases of employees rejecting SPs with cloud ‘shadow IT’ (Rials, 2021). In those instances, auxiliary measures are created to work around central SPs with ambitions to meet frictionless business goals. In so doing, the shadow IT seeks optimum balance between productivity and SP. Except, to IT-it-yourself is widely considered an enterprise cybersecurity risk of its own kind (Klotz, et al., 2019).

 

 

3.   Conclusion and Recommendations

 

3.1 Conclusion

 

This paper tendered a SP literature review encompassing threefold issues on its definitional scope, enforcement challenges, and workplace problems. In sum, it finds:

 

a)    SP a multifarious term among cybersecurity communities shares constitutional properties as a strategic written artefact whilst formal definitions allow standards to develop from SPFs.

 

b)    Enforcing SP faces obstacles using appropriate methods under a SPGM from manual to automated infrastructural controls and guaranteed compliance requiring monitoring technology.

 

c)    Measures contending ethics cause noncompliance, emergent technologies outpacing network defences yield new cyberattacks, and SP circumvention lessoning productivity bottlenecks is available in risky shadow IT.

 

3.2 Recommendations

 

SP is privy to cyberspace inasmuch to progressive social and ethical by-products it creates in ‘meatspace’. As problems arise, answers may be found in these areas:

 

a)    Relations between SP and cloud services and artificial intelligence incorporation to support SPF baselines to avoid reusable hacks (CheckPoint Research, 2020).   

 

b)    Enforcement efficacy in working from home trends (Irwin, 2021), SP jurisdictions over domestication inciting ‘living at work’ culture, and remote compliance.

 

c)    Value conflict resolution, pacing solutions covering responsive regulation (Renda, 2021), measures to induce work performance and innovation (Möller, 2020).

 

Closing gaps SP has with people it depends on to work efficiently will of course face the gravities of COVID-19 as we rethink our relationship with cybersecurity in unpredictable times.
